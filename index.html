Introduction

About Me as a Reader

Literature has always fascinated me as a window into different worlds, perspectives, and human experiences. Before enrolling in Great Books, my reading was primarily for personal enjoyment and escape. However, this course transformed my approach to literature, teaching me to engage with texts analytically and thoughtfully. I learned to examine the deeper meanings behind stories—the symbols, themes, and social contexts that give literature its lasting power. What attracted me to this course was the chance to explore foundational works that have shaped literary tradition and continue to influence contemporary thought. My goal was to develop stronger critical thinking skills, broaden my cultural understanding, and learn how literature reflects and critiques the human condition. Through this journey, I've grown from a passive reader into an active interpreter who questions, analyzes, and connects literary works to broader human experiences.

About the Course

Great Books is an intellectually enriching course that examines influential literary works spanning various cultures, time periods, and genres. The course challenges students to move beyond surface-level reading and engage with texts through multiple critical lenses, including psychoanalytic, feminist, Marxist, and reader-response approaches. By studying canonical literature alongside diverse perspectives, we explore fundamental questions about human nature, social structures, morality, and the power of storytelling. This course emphasizes close reading, analytical writing, and thoughtful discussion, encouraging students to develop their own interpretations while considering historical and cultural contexts. Through Great Books, I've gained valuable tools for literary analysis and a deeper appreciation for how literature both reflects and shapes our understanding of the world.

Literary Analysis 2

Archetypal Criticism The Foolish Crane and the Mongoose by Panchatantra

Summary

The Foolish Crane and the Mongoose This fable tells the story of a crane whose chicks are repeatedly eaten by a poisonous black snake living at the base of her nesting tree. Grieving her loss, the crane encounters a clever crab who offers a plan to defeat the snake. Following the crab’s advice, the crane lures a mongoose to the snake’s lair by leaving a trail of food. The mongoose kills the snake as expected. However, the solution soon backfires: the mongoose climbs the trees and begins attacking the cranes themselves, ultimately destroying them all. The story ends with the moral that solutions should not make problems worse, warning against actions that create greater harm than the original issue.

Analysis and Interpretation

Archetypal Elements in the Story

  • The Snake as the Villain – The black snake symbolizes danger, destruction, and a persistent threat to the innocent, similar to classic villain figures in folklore.
  • The Crane as the Victim/Hero – The crane represents those who suffer but try to seek a solution. Though vulnerable, she takes action to protect her offspring.
  • The Crab as the Trickster/Helper – The crab plays a cunning role, giving advice that is partly helpful but also self-serving. This figure shows how guidance can be deceptive, reflecting the “trickster” archetype.
  • The Mongoose as the Unintended Consequence – The mongoose represents the unpredictable results of using force or cunning. Although it solves the initial problem, it creates a bigger disaster.
  • Using this archetypal lens connects the story to a larger tradition of fables worldwide. It illustrates how villains, helpers, and heroes appear in many cultures, and how these roles help teach practical life lessons about caution, cleverness, and the unforeseen consequences of our actions.

Using the archetypal lens makes the story feel connected to many other tales across cultures. We recognize the snake, the hero and the helper because these character types appear in stories all over the world. Seeing these patterns helps us understand why the story is memorable: it teaches a simple but strong lesson about overcoming challenges using clever thinking rather than force.

Literary Analysis 3

Archetypal / Allegorical Lens Cupid and Psyche by Apuleius

Summary

Psyche is a princess so beautiful that people admire her more than the goddess of beauty. This makes the goddess Venus jealous. She orders her son Cupid to make Psyche fall in love with a monster, but instead, Cupid falls in love with her. Psyche is brought to a magical palace by invisible forces, where she lives with a husband she is not allowed to see. Although she is treated with love and comfort, she becomes curious. Her jealous sisters convince her to look at him. One night, she secretly shines a lamp on him and discovers that her husband is Cupid. Surprised, she accidentally hurts him, and Cupid runs away. Sad and desperate, Psyche travels across the land trying to find him. Venus forces her to complete several hard tasks to prove her worth. With the help of nature and the gods, Psyche succeeds in all of them. In the end, Cupid takes her to the gods, she becomes immortal, and they marry. Psyche becomes a goddess.

Analysis and Interpretation

Key archetypal and symbolic elements

  • Psyche as the Soul – The name “Psyche” means “soul.” Her journey from mortal life, through loss, exile, and trials, to finally becoming divine reflects the soul’s path toward growth and spiritual maturity.
  • Cupid as Love and Guide – Cupid’s love for Psyche represents a higher, guiding force that leads the soul toward growth and immortality. Their hidden and initially forbidden relationship shows the deep, inner connection between love and the soul beyond appearances or society’s rules.
  • Trials as Tests of Growth – The tasks given by Venus symbolize stages of testing and purification. Psyche’s patience, faith, and humility reflect the qualities needed for personal and spiritual development.
  • From Mortal to Divine – Psyche’s transformation from vulnerable human to immortal goddess symbolizes growth, endurance, and transcendence—a common theme in myths showing that struggle can lead to something greater.

Cupid and Psyche is more than a love story; it is an allegory for the soul’s journey. It explores love, doubt, suffering, growth, and transformation. The trials Psyche faces are not just plot points—they represent inner, psychological, or spiritual challenges. The story shows that love based on trust and faith can lead to deep personal change.

Literary Analysis 5

Marxist Criticism Kulakog and Tilmag a Philippine Folktale

Summary

The story of Kulakog and Tilmag is about two animals who live in the same place but act very differently. Tilmag is hardworking and knows how to find what she needs, while Kulakog is lazy and depends on others. One day, their differences become obvious. Tilmag works hard to gather food and prepare for the future, but Kulakog does nothing and simply benefits from Tilmag’s effort. In the end, Kulakog suffers because of her laziness. The story shows that hard work leads to success, while doing nothing leads to failure.

Analysis and Interpretation

Key Archetypal and Symbolic Elements

  • Kulakog as the Hero / Worker – Represents hard work, effort, and personal responsibility. He is the typical “worker” who provides for himself and others.
  • Tilmag as the Trickster / Dependent – Symbolizes laziness and taking advantage of others’ efforts instead of contributing.
  • Conflict Between Effort and Idleness – The struggle between Kulakog and Tilmag reflects broader social tensions between productive and non-productive members of society.
  • Consequences as a Moral Lesson – Tilmag’s suffering shows that rewards come from effort, emphasizing justice and accountability.

The story mirrors real-life social dynamics, where hard work can be overlooked and unfairness arises when some benefit without contributing. Kulakog and Tilmag is not just a moral tale about diligence; it also reflects ideas about fairness, society, and the value of labor.

Literary Analysis 6

Reader Response Criticism Morella by Edgar Allan Poe

Summary

Morella tells the story of a man who marries a very intelligent woman named Morella. She spends her time studying deep questions about life, death, and the soul. After a long illness, Morella dies, but before her death, she talks about the soul living on and being reborn. Later, the man remarries, and he and his new wife have a daughter. As the child grows, she begins to look and act exactly like Morella. In time, the girl also dies, and the narrator realizes that she was the return of Morella. The story shows how the past can haunt the present and explores themes of identity, obsession, and the idea of coming back after death.

Analysis and Interpretation

Key Archetypal and Symbolic Elements

  • Morella as the Mystic / Intellectual – Represents forbidden knowledge, obsession, and the mysteries of life and death.
  • The Daughter as Rebirth / Return – Symbolizes reincarnation, fate, and the cycle of life.
  • The House and Setting – The gothic imagery of the home and tomb reflects the narrator’s fears and emotional struggles.
  • Themes of Identity and Legacy – Morella and her daughter show how the past and unresolved connections can continue to influence life beyond death.

Using a Reader Response lens, the story highlights how the reader’s emotions, imagination, and personal interpretation interact with the text. Morella creates suspense, horror, and fascination by letting readers experience fear, grief, and obsession through the narrator’s perspective.

Literary Analysis 4

Psychoanalytic Criticism A Rose for Emily by William Faulkner

Summary

A Rose for Emily is about Emily Grierson, a lonely woman who lives in an old, decaying house in her town. After her father dies, she shuts herself off from other people. She later spends time with a man named Homer Barron, but their relationship remains uncertain and strange. Emily refuses to accept change and continues to hold on to the past. When she dies, the townspeople enter her home and make a shocking discovery, Homer’s body is hidden in one of the rooms, and it becomes clear that Emily had been sleeping beside it for many years. The story shows how isolation, obsession, and a strict upbringing can destroy a person’s life.

Analysis and Interpretation

Key Archetypal and Symbolic Elements

  • Emily as the Repressed Self – Represents repression, unresolved grief, and clinging to the past.
  • The House – Symbolizes Emily’s mind and the stagnation of her life; its decay reflects her psychological decline.
  • Homer Barron’s Corpse – Shows Emily’s inability to let go and the dangers of obsession and denial.
  • The Rose (in the title) – Suggests secrecy, remembrance, or pity for Emily; it acknowledges her tragic inner life.

Using a Psychoanalytic lens, the story explores Emily’s unconscious mind, her repression, and unresolved conflicts. Emily’s actions are shaped by childhood trauma, fear of abandonment, and her struggle to accept reality while clinging to her desires.

Literary Analysis 1

Formalist Criticism Psalm 23 and Job 38, The Bible

Summary

Psalm 23 shows trust in God as a caring shepherd who leads, protects, and provides for his people. Even when walking “through the valley of the shadow of death,” the believer does not fear, because God gives guidance, safety, and comfort.

Job 38 is part of God’s answer to Job after his suffering. God asks Job questions about the world to show how large and complex creation is. Through these questions about nature, the universe, and living creatures, God reminds Job that His power and wisdom are far greater than human understanding.

Analysis and Interpretation

Key Archetypal and Symbolic Elements

  • Shepherd (Psalm 23) – Represents guidance, care, and protection; a common symbol of leadership and nurturing.
  • Valley of Shadows (Psalm 23) – Symbolizes life’s challenges, danger, or death.
  • God’s Questions (Job 38) – Represent divine wisdom, the unknowable, and human limits.
  • Nature Imagery (Job 38) – Mountains, seas, and weather show order, beauty, and power, reflecting the complexity of the universe and God’s design.

A formalist approach focuses on how literary devices—like structure, imagery, tone, and metaphor—create meaning. By looking closely at the text itself, we see how the careful use of language delivers spiritual and emotional impact, making messages of guidance, trust, and humility powerful and timeless.

Summary of Learning

01

Collective Insights on Human Experience

Across the six texts I studied, a common idea about human life is the ongoing struggle between what people want and the forces that affect their lives. Whether the stories are about love, loss, identity, or inner conflict, they show how people try to understand themselves while dealing with life’s uncertainty. Many characters face moments that test their beliefs, push them to change, or make them confront difficult truths about who they are. Themes like resilience, weakness, moral choices, and emotional struggle appear again and again. These texts remind us that human experience is not simple. It is shaped by both our inner feelings and the outside world, which together influence what we do, what we believe, and who we become.

02

Society and Culture

These texts also show how strongly society and culture affect the way people think and act. By using different places and time periods, the stories reveal how social rules, class divisions, traditions, and institutions shape what people can or cannot do. Many of the works show unfair differences between genders, social classes, or cultural groups, where some people benefit from privilege while others struggle against unfair systems. At the same time, the texts question the rules that society often treats as normal or unquestionable. By showing characters who follow, resist, or are harmed by these rules, the stories help us understand how culture can guide us but also limit us. Together, these texts show that literature not only reflects the society that creates it, but also invites readers to think critically about the world they live in.

03

Theme: The Struggle for Identity

A common theme in many of the stories is the characters' search for who they truly are. Whether they are questioning their place in society, dealing with inner conflicts, or rethinking their beliefs, many characters face moments that make them confront their true selves. This theme shows up when characters resist outside expectations, challenge traditional roles, or try to understand their purpose. The stories show that identity is not fixed; it grows from personal experiences, relationships, and the pressures of the world around them.

04

Theme: Power and Social Inequality

Another important theme is how power and social inequality affect people's lives. Many stories show how class, gender, and authority shape choices and limit freedom. Characters often struggle between those in power and those who are marginalized. The texts reveal how society creates systems that favor a few while leaving others at a disadvantage, and how people respond by following, resisting, or being affected by these systems.

05

Theme: Change Through Conflict

Many stories also show how conflict whether inside a person, between people, or in society leads to change. Characters face challenges that make them adapt, grow, or see life differently. These struggles help them understand justice, responsibility, truth, or human nature. In these stories, change is not sudden; it comes gradually through effort, reflection, and resilience.

06

Key Realizations

Studying these texts together helped me see how literature shows patterns of human behavior that appear across different times and cultures. One important realization is that, even though the stories come from different backgrounds, they all explore universal questions about identity, power, and morality. I was surprised to see how much society influences personal choices and how characters often face silent struggles shaped by rules and expectations they cannot escape. This changed my view that literature is only about individual stories; I now understand that each story also reflects larger social forces. I also learned that conflict often drives growth, showing that real change comes from challenges rather than comfort. Overall, reading these works helped me better understand the complexities of human life and the many things that shape who we become.

Final Reflection

01

Evolution of My Literary Understanding

This Great Books course has fundamentally transformed my approach to literary analysis and interpretation. Initially, my reading focused primarily on surface narratives and plot progression. However, through sustained engagement with diverse theoretical frameworks, I developed the capacity to analyze literature with greater sophistication and depth. I learned to examine characterization, symbolic systems, thematic complexity, and underlying ideological messages embedded within texts. The various critical lenses demonstrated that literature operates on multiple interpretive levels, each revealing different dimensions of meaning. I evolved from a passive consumer of stories into an active, questioning reader who interrogates texts, considers authorial choices, and connects literary works to broader philosophical, social, and psychological frameworks. This course enhanced my ability to identify significant literary details, construct evidence-based interpretations, and understand how literature illuminates fundamental aspects of human experience and social organization.

02

Most Challenging Lens

The Psychoanalytic lens was the hardest for me. It made me think about the characters’ hidden feelings and motivations, like fears or desires they might not even understand themselves. At first, it was confusing to connect these ideas to the story events. By rereading stories like A Rose for Emily and focusing on clear examples of obsession or fear, I learned how to use this lens. It helped me notice the inner struggles of characters and understand their actions more deeply.

03

Most Insightful Lens

The Reader Response lens was the most helpful. It showed me that reading is not just about the story itself, but also about how I respond to it. While reading Morella, my feelings of fear and curiosity shaped my understanding. This lens taught me to pay attention to my reactions and reminded me that different readers can interpret the same story in different ways. I plan to keep using this approach in the future.

04

Impact on Thinking, Values, and Worldview

Reading these Great Books helped me think about life, society, and human nature in new ways. Stories like Cupid and Psyche and The Crow and the Snake made me reflect on love, intelligence, and morality. I also learned about fairness, resilience, and spiritual growth from texts like Psalm 23 and Job 38. These stories made me more thoughtful, empathetic, and aware of human struggles. I now see literature as a way to better understand people, society, and myself.

05

Final Thoughts

This course was a valuable learning experience. It helped me think critically and reflect more deeply on stories. Using different lenses and reading a variety of texts showed me that stories are more than just entertainment—they teach lessons about life, human nature, and society. I will carry these skills and insights with me, not just in school, but in how I read, think, and understand the world in the future.
